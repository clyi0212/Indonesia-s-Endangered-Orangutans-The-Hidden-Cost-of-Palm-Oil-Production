Indonesia's Endangered Orangutans:
The Hidden Cost of Palm Oil Production

By Yi Wan

The study highlights how the profitable palm oil industry in Indonesia poses a growing threat to the survival of orangutans, putting them at risk of extinction. It underlines the pressing need for sustainable palm oil practices to safeguard these endangered animals and support local communities.

Palm oil is a widely used vegetable oil, ranking among the top three vegetable oils in production volume, along with soybean and rapeseed oil.

Palm oil is commonly used as an additive rather than a primary ingredient, making it easy to overlook its presence in many products. However, due to its widespread production and usage, it is a prevalent component in a diverse range of items.

In the last 60 years, our demand for palm oil has skyrocketed...

It is resulting in a surge in palm oil production from 1.52 million tonnes in 1961 to a whopping 75.99 million tonnes in 2020. Indonesia is the top producer, accounting for 59% of the world's total production in 2020. The country's palm oil production has seen a dramatic increase, rising from just 0.15 million tonnes in 1961 to 44.76 million tonnes in 2020.

However, this increase in palm oil production has come at a cost. The expansion of palm oil plantations has caused widespread destruction. In the period 2001-2019,9.79 million hectares of Indonesian forests were destroyed. The regions most affected were Sumatra and Kalimantan, which together accounted for 82.7% of the total forest loss.

A closer examination of the data revealed that over 30% of the cleared land in these regions had been converted into palm oil farms.

Orangutans, known as the "people of the forest" in Indonesian culture, share a remarkable 96.4% genetic similarity with humans. However, it is disheartening that the largest orangutan populations in Indonesia, both Bornean and Sumatran, are now endangered. The Bornean orangutan population is estimated at just 104,700, while the Sumatran orangutan population stands at around 14,613, and both numbers continue to dwindle.

The alarming decline in orangutan populations, encompassing both Bornean and Sumatran species, can be primarily attributed to the extensive destruction of tropical rainforests in Sumatra and Kalimantan. This destructive trend is largely fueled by the relentless expansion of oil palm plantations, causing severe fragmentation of orangutan habitats and placing their very existence in jeopardy.

Estimates suggest that industrial-scale plantations kill between 1,000 and 5,000 orangutans annually. How do oil palm plantations kill orangutans?
Palm oil companies commonly drain and burn peat orests to clear land for oil palm tree growth. This practice caused an uncontrollable fire in Kalimantan in 1997-98, lasting six months and killing up to 8,000 orangutans.
Habitat destruction deprives orangutans of their food sources, compelling them to search for food in isolated and dangerous areas of plantations. It makes them susceptible to being hunted as pests or dying of starvation.
Oil palm companies view orangutans as pests and deliberately set policies to poach them, according to the Center for Orangutan Conservation (COP). Pest control hired by palm oil companies kills any wildlife, including orangutans, that damages oil palm trees and often show the company the hand of the killed orangutan as proof of their success.

Given the devastating impact that palm oil production has on orangutans, you may be wondering why we don't simply stop using it altogether.

Besides it is true that palm oil has become an integral part of our daily lives, there is an important consideration that warrants attention. Switching away from palm oil to other oil crops could potentially result in fourther loss of biodiversity.

Palm oil's remarkable efficiency distinguishes it as an incredibly productive crop, outperforming other major oilseed crops in oil production. To put it in perspective, sunflower oil, which is the second highest yielding crop, produces only 24.74% of what palm oil yields per hectare. This exceptional efficiency not only boosts the cost-effectiveness of palm oil, but also enhances its appeal among manufacturers and consumers, making it a favored choice.

Furthermore, it is important to acknowledge that the palm oil industry plays a significant role in providing employment opportunities to over 4.42 million people.These workers are also primarily concentrated in the regions of Sumatra and Kalimantan, and they are involved in various stages of production, including planting, harvesting, processing, and transportation.

As we can see, this is a complex issue with multiple facets, lacking easy solutions. Halting palm oil production could result in significant job losses, while transitioning to alternative vegetable oils may lead to further habitat destruction. However, it is evident that we must take action to protect orangutans and their habitats, while also supporting sustainable palm oil practices that benefit both local communities and the environment.

Non-feasible measures and consequences

So, in this context,
producing sustainable palm oil indeed becomes a feasible solution...

Sustainable palm oil production utilizes already-cleared land, thereby avoiding further deforestation. This mindful approach helps to minimize the impact on the environment and wildlife, while also supporting local communities.

But how do we know if a product uses sustainable palm oil?

As consumers, our role is indeed powerful; The choices we make when purchasing directly shape the supply and demand chain, and in turn, influence supplier behavior.

To identify products containing sustainable palm oil, look for the RSPO (Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil) logo during your shopping endeavors - the gold standard in sustainable palm oil production. This symbol signifies adherence to eight strict RSPO principles, assuring that palm oil producers follow these critical standards.

However, progress has been slow. As of 2020, RSPO certified sustainable palm oil constitutes a mere 19% of the total global palm oil supply. This illustrates the struggle that sustainable palm oil has faced in gaining widespread acceptance.

Therefore, If labor welfare in Indonesia's palm oil industry or orangutan survival are issues that resonate with you, opt for products bearing the RSPO logo when you shop. If you cannot find any products with the logo, don't hesitate to reach out to manufacturers, advocating for the use of sustainable palm oil.

Together, let's do our part for our environment, wildlife, and future through mindful buying decisions.
